Arm Path / Elbow Spiral

Important to know before continuing:

Pronation is the action of turning your palm downward or backward. If you're standing with your arms at your sides, pronation would rotate your palms to face behind you. For a pitcher, this typically occurs during the later part of the throwing motion, as the ball is released.

Supination is the opposite movement - turning your palm upward or forward. If your arms are at your sides, supination would rotate your palms to face forward. In pitching, this usually happens earlier in the throwing motion, as the pitcher prepares to throw.

The elbow spiral is an important concept in pitching mechanics that focuses on how a pitcher's arm moves during delivery. It involves coordinating the rotation and positioning of the arm in a specific pattern to create a more efficient throwing motion. The key idea is to match external rotation with supination, and internal rotation with pronation, creating a spiral-like movement of the arm.

This technique aims to improve a pitcher's arm action by making it shorter and more effective. While originally proposed by a Japanese coach, it has been adapted and refined over time. Instead of relying on excessive pitching practice, coaches teach this method through targeted drills and exercises that help pitchers internalize the desired movement pattern.

Glove Hand

The glove hand is a very important and underrated aspect of a pitcher's motion. Here is some important info about our glove hand:

Yoshinobu Yamamoto was up nearly 2 mph one day, averaging 97+ mph. He reportedly made a mechanical tweak prior to this start. Check the glove arm before (left) and after (right).

The glove arm can function to increase torso rotational velocity, similar to how a figure skater will spin faster when they bring their arms closer to the center at the right time.

We not only want to get the glove arm in the same plane of rotation as the trunk, but also to delay this unwinding of the glove arm until the right time, which functions to help hold the trunk closed as long as possible in the delivery.

Holding this exaggerated glove side internal rotation torque is a common way to delay the front side and lead to a more powerful unload from the torso when it does finally get pulled through. If the front side (aka torso) opens too early, it could lead to cutting the ball and velocity going down.

Cutting the Ball

Addressing a cut-fastball can be a process. Generally, habitual movement patterns have set in and it will require a good amount of dedication and hard work. But it is definitely still possible! Still, there will be a process of trial and retrial until you begin to see results.

Something we will try next practice is integration of the taped baseball method, I'll bring the baseball.

You can take some duct tape and wrap a line around the baseball. This tape should be placed in between the fingers wherever the 4-seam fastball grip is held. This will increase Ben's feel with an actual baseball, as both you and Ben will be able to see the ball's line spin around the 12 - 3 o'clock area. (anything outside of that will be considered cutting it or having inefficient spin)

I feel like the best fix for Ben on cutting the ball will be pitch grip, since our drills and changes we worked on during practice (arm path cleanup and front side fix) fixed most of the problem, now it's up to grip.

Cut fastballs generally occur because the athlete's middle finger is too far on the outside of the ball, or the fingers are off of the seams causing the ball to slip, as we can see in this image.

This causes an increase in gyro degree and causes the index finger to generate a high portion of gyro spin. Compared to a 4-seam fastball, the middle and pointer finger are positioned more in the middle of the baseball and leave the ball simultaneously resulting in more pure backspin.

IMPORTANT NOTE: There is no set in stone way to grip your fastball, but the general trend is to have fingers closer together, but we will work together next session to find a grip that is comfortable for Ben.
